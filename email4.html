Celebrating Two Decades of Discovery

Did you know Georgia is home to the world's busiest airport and North America's largest blackwater wetland? Or that the Cabbage Patch Kids craze was born here? Georgia has produced over two dozen Pulitzer Prize-winners, seven Olympic gold medalists, and two Nobel laureates. When we say Georgia influences everything, we mean it—from the fashion entrepreneur who dressed First Ladies, to the birthplace of country music recording and southern hip hop's center of gravity, to the artists whose work has adorned everything, including book covers, album covers, and the walls of the Smithsonian. 

For the past 20 years, the New Georgia Encyclopedia (NGE) has captured all these stories and more. In it, you can find the state's most surprising facts alongside turning points in our nation's history, from the Civil War to the Civil Rights Movement.

Since its launch in 2004—the same year Facebook changed how people connect—the NGE has transformed how educators, students, historians, journalists, and curious readers learn about our state. And NGE remains a comprehensive and trusted resource, committed to the idea that Georgia's history and culture are public goods that belong to us all.

Georgia Students Honored by GHRAC

Two of our outstanding National History Day Georgia students will be recognized by the Georgia Historical Records Advisory Council (GHRAC) at their annual awards ceremony on October 21.

Congratulations to Joshua Buchanan from General Ray Davis Middle School in Stockbridge and Madelyn Reardanz from Ola High School in McDonough! Both students earned an Award for Excellence in Student Research Using Historical Records. For their projects, Joshua produced a documentary on the 1974 super tornado outbreak, and Madelyn created an exhibit on the election of Atlanta mayor Maynard Jackson.

Revisit the Legacy of President Carter

With support from a Georgia Humanities grant, Recollecting Carter dropped its final episode on October 1, 2024—just in time for Jimmy Carter's 100th birthday. Hosted by Clayton State University Professor Jason Guthrie, the podcast features interviews with historians and authors who explore Carter's life, legacy, and impact on our state and nation. This Month in Georgia History

A snapshot of October in Georgia history, courtesy of the New Georgia Encyclopedia.
Explore the NGE

In October 1765, naturalists John and William Bartram discovered a small grove of trees with white flowers along the southern reaches of the Altamaha River; the species became commonly known as the Franklin tree.

The state capital moved from Louisville to Milledgeville in October 1807.

Jimmy Carter, former U.S. president and Georgia governor, celebrated his 100th birthday on October 1. He was born in 1924 in Plains. 

Maynard Jackson was elected mayor of Atlanta in October 1973, becoming the first Black mayor of a major southern city at the age of 35.
