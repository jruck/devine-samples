Save the Date for Ocmulgee Rising: 
A Celebration of Muscogee Creativity with Joy Harjo

When: April 24, 2025 @ 11:00 am
 Where: Middle Georgia State University, Macon Campus 

Georgia Humanities invites you to join us for Ocmulgee Rising: A Celebration of Muscogee Creativity featuring Joy Harjo—acclaimed poet, musician, and the first Native American U.S. Poet Laureate.

Through word and song, Harjo will share stories and reflections that connect past and present, honoring the legacy of the Muscogee people in Georgia. This event is presented in partnership with Middle Georgia State University, the Ocmulgee Mounds Association, the Georgia Council for the Arts, and the National Humanities Center.

RSVP today to save your seat. We hope to see you for an unforgettable morning of art and humanity!

New on Our Bookshelf!
Civil Sights: Sweet Auburn, a Journey through Atlanta's National Treasure

Once the wealthiest Black neighborhood in the world, Atlanta’s Sweet Auburn Historic District remains a globally significant landmark of the civil rights movement. Yet, as the city continues to grow around it, many of its most important spaces face decline and demolition. In Civil Sights, author, preservationist, and cultural developer Gene Kansas shines a light on the neighborhood’s architectural and cultural legacy, offering an illustrated and historic guidebook designed to educate and inspire action.

Featuring artwork from Atlanta architect and Georgia Tech professor Clay Kiningham and a foreword from author and journalist Gary M. Pomerantz, Civil Sights explores locations such as the first integrated fire station and the Butler Street YMCA that served as Atlanta’s “Black City Hall.” Along the way, Kansas unspools a compelling history of Sweet Auburn’s spaces and their significance—past and present—while posing pressing questions about preservation and historical accountability. Civil Sights is published by UGA Press in association with Georgia Humanities.

This Month in Georgia History

A snapshot of February in Georgia history, courtesy of the New Georgia Encyclopedia.
Explore the NGE

On February 12, 1733, James Oglethorpe founded the colony of Georgia and subsequently laid out the plans for the city of Savannah.

Elias Boudinot published the first issue of the Cherokee Phoenix, a newspaper printed at New Echota in Cherokee and English, on February 21, 1828.

On February 22, 1908, librarian, educator, and civil rights activist Annie L. McPheeters was born. 

Helen Douglas Mankin became the first woman from Georgia elected to the U.S. Congress on February 12, 1946.
