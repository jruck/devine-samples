Invest in Community and Connection

As the season of giving and gratitude approaches, we’re reflecting on the power of the humanities to connect, inspire, and bring us closer together. The humanities—our stories, histories, and cultures—are more than a collection of things we study. They’re an expression of who we are.

The humanities shape our lives in ways big and small. If you’ve recently read a book or listened to an audiobook, visited a museum, library, or historic site, shared a family story or tradition, or researched the past to better understand the present, the humanities have been a part of your life this year. 

But beyond enriching our individual experiences, the humanities have the potential to transform our communities. In 2024, with the support of our donors, Georgia Humanities:

  • Reached over 54,000 Georgia students and educators through our National History Day program
  • Awarded $68,500 in grants to 32 Georgia organizations
  • Welcomed 25% more visitors to our digital New Georgia Encyclopedia
  • Supported 16 local book festivals and literacy initiatives 

Together, our programs touched every region of our state, engaging thousands of people directly and reaching millions more online. This Giving Tuesday, December 3, we invite you to join us in creating a future for Georgia built on curiosity, connection, and understanding.

Please mark your calendar for Giving Tuesday, or make your gift now. Your contribution of any size makes our work possible.

Georgia Teacher Selected to Research Stories from Veterans of the Korean War

Congratulations to Mr. Chance Giddens of Troup County High School in LaGrange, who is among 48 teachers selected for the new National History Day (NHD) program: “Silent Heroes: Untold Stories from the Korean War.” Funded by the Veterans Legacy Program at the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, this unique project brings together teachers and students from across the country to honor Veterans through research and storytelling. Mr. Giddens and his students will meet regularly with professional historians and mentors as they work to create Silent Hero profiles of their selected veterans: U.S. Army Corporal Shigeru Ito and U.S. Air Force Corporal Shirley Jean Tinker Thomas. Read the full press release to learn more about this program.

Students Gather for Research Roundup 

Earlier this month, local students and teachers got a jumpstart on their NHD Georgia research projects during a Research Roundup event at LaGrange College. During the event, participants had access to the college library's resources and the opportunity to review their work with NHD Georgia staff. 

Students across the state are gearing up for the NHD Georgia regional contest season, which kicks off in February.

NEH Grant Opportunity for Communities Impacted by Hurricanes


The National Endowment for the Humanities (NEH) Chair’s Disaster Recovery Grants program is now accepting applications from organizations in communities affected by Hurricanes Helene and Milton. These grants support the recovery of humanities collections, including rare books, documents, historical artifacts, and more. Applications are being accepted on a rolling basis until April 25, 2025. For full details, please visit the NEH website.

This Month in Georgia History

A snapshot of November in Georgia history, courtesy of the New Georgia Encyclopedia.
Explore the NGE

In November 1790, a charter was granted to Congregation Mickve Israel in Savannah, the oldest Jewish congregation in the South.

Construction began on the new capitol building in Atlanta in November 1884.

In November 1942, Frank Sinkwich, a halfback for the UGA football team, won the Heisman Trophy.

The Albany Movement began in November 1961 after demonstrators were arrested for attempting to integrate the municipal bus station in Albany.
