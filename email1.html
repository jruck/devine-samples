The Women Who Move Us and Make Us

From political trailblazers to literary icons, Georgia women have made a lasting impact on our state and nation. In honor of Women’s History Month, we invite you to explore their stories in the New Georgia Encyclopedia, including:

Frankie Welch – Rome fashion designer who dressed First Ladies
Shay Youngblood – Celebrated Columbus author and playwright
Moina Belle Michael – Georgia educator and WWI Remembrance Advocate
Grace Towns Hamilton – First Black woman elected to the Georgia General Assembly

Learn about these influential women and more who made Georgia history in the New Georgia Encyclopedia.

Sharing Our Impact on Capitol Hill

Earlier this month, staff and board members from Georgia Humanities traveled to Washington, D.C., as part of the annual Humanities on the Hill advocacy week organized by the Federation of State Humanities Councils. There, they met with our state's elected officials to share how funding for the humanities makes a real difference in the lives of Georgians—by preserving our state's stories, supporting our schools, strengthening local economies, and bringing communities together.

Many thanks to Georgia's Senators, Representatives, and their staff members for taking the time to meet with our team and learn more about our statewide impact. 

Want to show your support for Georgia Humanities and the National Endowment for the Humanities? Consider contacting your representative, sharing your humanities story on social, or making a gift today.

This Month in Georgia History

A snapshot of March in Georgia history, courtesy of the New Georgia Encyclopedia.
Explore the NGE

“Georgia Wonder” Annie Abbott gave her first performance in March 1885.

In March 1912, eighteen girls held the first Girl Scouts meeting at the home of Juliette Gordon Low in Savannah.

Influential writer Flannery O’Connor was born a century ago on March 25, 1925.       

On March 21, 1939, track and field Olympian Martha Hudson was born in Eastman.

Wesleyan College hosted the first induction ceremony for Georgia Women of Achievement in March 1992.
